Ebola outbreak in North Kivu

This site contains up-to-date data on the current Ebola outbreak in North Kivu in the Democratic Republic of Congo. The data is collected by the MoH in DRC and is available from the humanitarian data exchange. Data until the {{date}} is currently available.

Explanations

We use a mathematical model to estimate the reproduction number and to predict the future cases. A more detailed explanation of the model follows below.

The reproductive number,\(R\), of a disease tells us how many new cases one case will infect. It depends on many factors, but gives us an idea of how the disease is progressing. When R is less than one we expect the outbreak to die out and when R is greater than one we expect the outbreak to continue. The larger R is the more cases we would expect in the future. The estimation of R above is based on work by Cori et al 2013

We use the mathematical model of the disease to predict the expected number of cases of Ebola over the next 3 weeks. We take the latest estimated reproduction number and use that to simulate 1000 possible trajectories the number of cases could increase over the next three weeks. Mathematical Model

The mathematical model used to estimate \(R\) and to predict future cases of Ebola is based on calculating a rate of expected cases every day and then using a Poisson distribution to estimate the number of cases. See references below for more information. The daily expected rate, \(\lambda_t\), changes over time and is calculated from the reproduction number and the force of infection . $$ \lambda_t = R_t \sum_s I_s w_{t - s}, $$ where \(I_s\) is the incidence rates and \(w\) is the serial interval distribution . The serial interval is the time from the onset of disease in the primary case until the onset in the secondary case. As this time is not constant we model it with a probability distribution. From previous data on Ebola we use a Gamma Distribution with mean of about 15 days and a standard deviation of about 9 days to model the serial interval distribution.

For more details about this model see:

  • Paper describing the model and how to estimate R by Cori et al. 2013
  • Blog post describing the model with R code by Thibaut Jombart
